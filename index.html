1The irreducible variance floorReproduces the paper · Eq. 2, Box 3

A replication project reports the fraction of studies that “replicated,” μ̂ = X/m. If the studies are not exact copies of each other, their true replicability rates vary — captured by the intraclass correlation ρ. Heterogeneity puts a floor on how precise the reported rate can ever be: past it, adding studies buys you almost nothing.

This is the paper’s benchmark model: it assumes the m studies share one latent replicability rate (exchangeable) and that a value of ρ is known or imported. The panel then shows the sampling distribution of X/m under that assumption. It is a diagnostic of what a given ρ would imply — not a claim that raw one-verdict-per-study data reveal this shape (under the operational model of §4 the observed counts look like a plain Binomial).
Intuition for the floor: the benchmark model is like flipping one randomly-drawn coin m times — more flips pin down that coin, but it’s still a single draw from the purse, so your estimate of the average bias stays a fixed distance (the floor) from the truth. The operational model is like flipping many coins once each, where the average does converge. To beat the floor you need more coins, not more flips.

5What this means for the real headline numbersReproduces the paper · RPP, SCORE, ML4

Each project’s size with an assumed heterogeneity, through the effective-sample-size formula me = m / [1+(m−1)ρ]. For RPP and SCORE the ρ values are illustrative (Box 3 of the paper uses ρ ≈ 0.1–0.2 as examples); for ML4 they are the paper’s own auxiliary-model estimates. For ML4 the binary headline (“1 of 17 replicated”, ≈6%) and the diagnostic mean μ = 0.565 are different things: the latter comes from the paper’s continuous effect-size (auxiliary) model and is what the paper uses to place ML4 on its heterogeneity scale. At that μ and ρ = 0.175, the benchmark sampling interval for X/m spans 0.12–0.94 — nearly the whole scale — and the 17 sites carry the weight of only ~2–5 exact replications.

7The coin purse, made exact: fair coins vs rigged coinsBeyond the paper · our analogy

Your analogy, sharpened. Give every coin a hidden bias φ — its chance of landing heads — and read “heads” as “this study replicated.” You never see φ; you get one flip per coin. The rigged purse (two-headed and two-tailed coins) and the fair purse (every coin ~50/50) share the same average bias, so they produce the same flips. The average is knowable; the individual coins are not.

8Where does a replication rate come from?Beyond the paper · a complementary argument

What a replication rate actually tracks. Each tested hypothesis is a true effect with some probability — the base rate. Only significant originals get published, so the pool that gets replicated is a mix of real effects and false positives, and its quality (the share that are real, PPV) is set by literature properties: the base rate and how rigorously the originals were run. When the replications are well-powered (real effects comfortably exceed the SESOI), the rate ends up tracking PPV closely — so it does carry information about the share of real findings. What it still can’t separate are two different literatures behind the same PPV.

This base-rate model is a separate, classic argument (Ioannidis-style), not part of Devezer & Buzbas. It complements them: it shows what a rate does track, while the paper explains the limits of reading it as a verdict. Note this model has no effect heterogeneity (every real effect replicates at the same power) — the spread/ρ story is §§1&4.

9Does the “effective replications” claim apply to RPP-style projects?Our analysis · questions one of the paper’s claims

Sections 1–8 reproduce the paper. This one is our own argument, kept separate, about a single result — the “effective number of replications” in §§1 and 5 — for the common kind of project that replicates many different results once each (RPP, SCORE).

What the paper says

It defines two models and, for a project’s headline rate, reports the benchmark effective sample size me = m/[1+(m−1)ρ]: RPP’s 100 replications “carry the inferential weight of ~9 exact ones” at ρ=0.10. It states this conditionally — “if the benchmark model is the correct description” — and notes that under the operational model (one verdict per experiment) the effective sample size is exactly m, with the caveat that binary data can’t reveal which model holds.

Where we’d push

RPP and SCORE replicate many distinct results once each — the operational structure (each result its own rate), not the benchmark (all results sharing one latent rate). Heterogeneity among independent results is not intraclass correlation: with one verdict per result the count is exactly Binomial(m, μ), so the average replicability is estimated with ordinary precision and every study is fully informative — me = m. The “≈9 effective” figure rests on the benchmark’s shared-latent dependence, which a spread of distinct effects does not by itself create.

The strongest reply from the paper’s authors — and ours

Them: the 36% is not the average of a sampling scheme; it is read as a replicability property of a literature or of underlying results. That target needs a model of latent heterogeneity and dependence; the benchmark is the idealised diagnostic for it, and the binomial precision is illusory because it cannot see ρ.

Us: granted — but then me ≈ 9 is a conditional sensitivity calculation under an added shared-latent model, a quantity about that model rather than about the RPP data. It carries weight only with that dependence defended for a set of distinct effects.

What holds with no benchmark assumption: ρ — the spread of replicability across results — is unrecoverable from one verdict each; the aggregate’s meaning is composition-dependent; per-result “replicable / not” rests on a single verdict. Those are the paper’s durable points. The effective-replications strand is the part that turns on a modelling choice, and for many-targets-once projects we read the average as estimable.
